Gauteng Apartment Rental Market: A Feature-Based Price Variation Analysis

Introduction

This project focuses on apartment price variation across four cities in Gauteng: Pretoria, Sandton, Johannesburg, and Midrand. We explore attributes such as the location of apartments; the number of bedrooms, bathrooms and parking spaces each apartment has; the floor size of the apartment; and the estate agent each apartment complex belongs to. The aim is to understand how each feature influences price differences across apartments.

Business Question

Does location and estate agent influence apartment rental prices across Gauteng? And to what extent do internal apartment features, number of bedrooms, bathrooms, parking spaces and floor size, collectively and individually account for monthly rental price variation?

Tools and Methods

The data was collected by creating a web scraper in Python for Property24 listings across four major cities in Gauteng. The dataset was cleaned and explored in MySQL where descriptive statistics (mean, median, standard deviation and interquartile range) were computed to understand the distribution of rental prices. The individual effect of each feature on the price was measured using simple linear regression, Pearson’s correlation, Kruskal-Wallis test and the posthoc Dunn's test, while the combined effect of all features was measured using multiple linear regression in Python.

Findings

  • Combined, all six observed attributes in this dataset account for about 59.8% of monthly price variation across apartment listings. Unobserved features, such as amenities, apartment furnishings, and extras, could account for the remaining 40.2%.
  • There is a very inconsistent relationship (0.23) between bedrooms and monthly price. This indicates that bedrooms only account for about 5.29% of price variation between listings. When price responds to bedrooms, it usually drives up by R3 481.47.
  • There is a moderately consistent relationship (0.48) between bathrooms and monthly price. This indicates that bathrooms account for about 23% of price variation between listings. When price does respond to bathrooms, it usually drives up by R8 211.95 in this dataset.
  • There is very low correlation (0.22) between parking spaces and monthly prices, which indicates that the number of parking spaces only accounts for around 4.84% of price variation between listings. Each additional parking space drives prices up by R408.71.
  • There is a fragile correlation (0.13) between the floor size and monthly prices, which indicates that the floor size only accounts for around 1.69% of price variation between listings. When price does respond to floor size, it usually drives up by R53.52.
  • After running the Kruskal-Wallis Test on the cities in the database, we can conclude that location influences price variation across apartment listings. Furthermore, Sandton tends to have higher-priced apartments than the other three cities (Midrand, Pretoria and Johannesburg). The points are similar for estate agents.

Project Limitations

We couldn't include more cities in Gauteng because of their naturally low sample size that could introduce sample bias.

Conclusion

Monthly rental prices are typically shaped by intrinsic property features like bedroom count, bathroom availability, and floor size. So, it was particularly revealing to see not only how strongly these factors influence price variation, but also in what specific ways they do so. Beyond these physical attributes, we found strong evidence that price also depends meaningfully on location, confirming that apartments in cities like Sandton tend to command higher rents than comparable ones in Pretoria. Additionally, we observed systematic differences between estate agents, suggesting that agent-specific factors may also play a role in price setting.

Loan Approval Bias Assessment: Education & Employment Status

Introduction

The phenomenon of bias in banks has genuine effects for ordinary people. The objective of this project aimed to provide an answer to a fundamental yet crucial question: are loan approvals in this dataset influenced by an applicant's self-employment or degree, or are choices solely based on creditworthiness?

The business question

Does education status or employment type meaningfully influence loan approval outcomes, or are approvals determined primarily by financial creditworthiness?

Tools and methods

MySQL was used to study the dataset and compute acceptance rates across four categories of applicants: graduates, non-graduates, self-employed, and non-self-employed. Crucially, prior to rendering any conclusions, the research adjusted for CIBIL score and income to be certain that any observed differences in approval rates were due to bias as opposed to the inherent relationship between financial status, work, and education.

Why controlling for CIBIL score matters

Graduates typically have higher incomes and stronger credit scores, which are factors that legitimately influence approval decisions. By adjusting for these variables first, the analysis isolates education and employment status as independent factors, providing a fairer and more honest picture. A naive comparison might show graduates getting approved more frequently than non-graduates and incorrectly conclude bias exists. Nonetheless, graduates typically earn more money and have better credit scores, both of which have a real impact on acceptance choices. The approach separates education and work status as independent factors by first adjusting for these variables, providing a more equitable and truthful image.

Key findings

The approval rates were astonishingly consistent for each of the four categories:

There was only a 0.47 percentage point difference between the approval rates of graduates (62.45%) and non-graduates (61.98%). The approval rate for self-employed applicants was 62.23%, compared to 62.20% for non-self-employed applicants—a mere 0.03 percentage points.

The data unambiguously indicates that loan acceptance outcomes in this dataset are based on creditworthiness rather than whether an applicant has a degree or owns their own business. Differences this tiny are not statistically significant. The data plainly shows that creditworthiness, rather than an applicant's degree or business ownership determines loan acceptance results in this dataset.
